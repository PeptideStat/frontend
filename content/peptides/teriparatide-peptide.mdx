Teriparatide is a peptide medicine with a very different evidence profile from
research-market "bone peptides." It is a recombinant fragment of human
parathyroid hormone, sold as the prescription products Forteo and Bonsity, and
it is one of the few drugs that actually builds new bone rather than only
slowing bone loss.

The peptide is the first 34 amino acids of the 84-amino-acid human parathyroid
hormone, which is why it is written as PTH(1-34). That N-terminal fragment
carries the biologically active region of the hormone, so the shortened peptide
reproduces the receptor signaling of full-length PTH while being easier to
manufacture by recombinant DNA technology.

This guide is educational and not medical advice. Teriparatide is a
prescription injectable that should be started, monitored, changed or stopped
only through qualified medical care. Nothing here is a dosing recommendation;
figures are reported from official labels and published trials.

For broader context, see [what peptides are](/peptides/what-are-peptides) and
the [peptide half-life guide](/peptides/peptide-half-life-explained). It can

[octreotide](/peptides/octreotide-peptide),

[oxytocin](/peptides/oxytocin-peptide). The shared lesson is that approved
peptides are narrow, indication-specific tools, not interchangeable wellness
compounds.

## How Intermittent PTH Builds Bone

Parathyroid hormone is the body's main minute-to-minute regulator of calcium.
It acts through the PTH1 receptor (also called the PTH/PTHrP receptor), which
sits on osteoblasts and on kidney cells. The biology has a famous paradox:
*continuous* high PTH exposure, as in hyperparathyroidism, drives net bone
breakdown, but *intermittent* exposure drives net bone formation.

Teriparatide exploits the second pattern. A single small subcutaneous dose once
a day produces a brief spike of PTH1 receptor activation that fades within
hours. According to the Forteo label, the peptide preferentially stimulates
osteoblastic activity over osteoclastic activity on both trabecular and
cortical bone surfaces. The result over months is increased bone formation,
improved bone microarchitecture and greater bone mass.

That dependence on intermittent dosing is why the route and schedule are not
incidental details. The same molecule delivered as a sustained high level would
be expected to remove bone rather than add it. This is a peptide where
pharmacokinetics define the therapy.

## Pharmacokinetics And Route

Teriparatide is given as a once-daily subcutaneous injection into the thigh or
abdominal wall, at a labeled dose of 20 micrograms per day. After subcutaneous
injection the serum concentration peaks quickly and then falls fast: the Forteo
label reports an apparent half-life of about 1 hour after subcutaneous dosing,
versus only a few minutes when measured after intravenous administration. The
difference reflects absorption from the injection site rather than a slower
clearance.

This very short exposure window is the point. A long-acting depot would defeat
the intermittent-signaling strategy, which is why teriparatide is delivered as a
daily pen rather than the monthly or six-month depots used for some other
peptide hormones. If half-life concepts are unfamiliar, the
[peptide half-life explainer](/peptides/peptide-half-life-explained) walks
through why a short half-life can be a feature, not a flaw.

## Approval Status And Indications

Teriparatide is approved, not research-only. Eli Lilly's Forteo received FDA
approval in November 2002, and a second product, Bonsity (developed by Pfenex
and commercialized by Alvogen), was approved in 2019 as a pharmaceutically
equivalent teriparatide injection.

The current Forteo label lists three indications, each framed around high
fracture risk:

- Treatment of postmenopausal women with osteoporosis at high risk for
  fracture.
- To increase bone mass in men with primary or hypogonadal osteoporosis at
  high risk for fracture.
- Treatment of men and women with glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis at high
  risk for fracture.

The "high risk for fracture" language matters. The label positions teriparatide
for people with a history of osteoporotic fracture, multiple risk factors, or
failure of or intolerance to other osteoporosis therapy, not as a general
bone-density supplement.

## Evidence, And Its Limits

The pivotal evidence is the Fracture Prevention Trial reported by Neer and
colleagues in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2001. That randomized,
placebo-controlled trial enrolled 1,637 postmenopausal women with prior
vertebral fractures. Compared with placebo, daily 20-mcg teriparatide reduced
the risk of new vertebral fractures by about 65 percent and new nonvertebral
fragility fractures by about 53 percent, while increasing lumbar spine and
femoral neck bone mineral density over a median of roughly 21 months.

The evidence base extends beyond postmenopausal women. The Saag and colleagues
trial in *NEJM* (2007) randomized 428 patients with glucocorticoid-induced
osteoporosis to teriparatide or alendronate and found teriparatide produced
greater bone-density gains and significantly fewer new vertebral fractures,
which supported the glucocorticoid indication. Combination and sequencing
studies, such as the Finkelstein *NEJM* trial pairing PTH with alendronate,
helped clarify that adding a potent antiresorptive at the same time can blunt
the anabolic response.

The honest limits are real. The Fracture Prevention Trial was stopped early
when rat carcinogenicity findings emerged, so the planned treatment duration was
truncated and long-term fracture data are thinner than the headline numbers
suggest. Efficacy and safety were not formally evaluated beyond 2 years of use.
And because teriparatide builds a reservoir of bone that is gradually lost after
stopping, it is generally followed by an antiresorptive agent to preserve the
gains, which means it is rarely a standalone, one-and-done therapy.

## Safety Issues

Teriparatide's safety profile follows from its mechanism and from its unusual
regulatory history.

| Safety issue | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Osteosarcoma signal (rat data) | Dose- and duration-dependent bone tumors appeared in rats, prompting an original boxed warning and a 2-year caution. |
| Boxed warning removed (2020) | Human surveillance of millions of users found no increased osteosarcoma risk; the FDA removed the boxed warning. |
| Baseline osteosarcoma risk groups | The label still advises avoiding use in people with open epiphyses, Paget disease, prior skeletal radiation, bone metastases or hereditary predisposition. |
| Transient hypercalcemia | Calcium can rise after dosing; avoid in pre-existing hypercalcemia and monitor accordingly. |
| Orthostatic hypotension | Transient drops in blood pressure can occur with early doses, so injections are given where the patient can sit or lie down. |
| Urolithiasis | Use cautiously in people with active or recent kidney stones. |
| 2-year lifetime caution | Use beyond 2 years should only be considered if the patient remains at or returns to high fracture risk. |

The osteosarcoma story is the centerpiece and is widely misunderstood. The
original concern came entirely from Fischer rats, which developed bone tumors
in a dose- and time-dependent way, while an 18-month primate study at high
exposure produced none. The FDA approved Forteo in 2002 with a boxed warning
and a 2-year limit, then required postmarketing surveillance. A 15-year US
Osteosarcoma Surveillance Study (Gilsenan and colleagues) found only three
exposed cases against 4.17 expected, a standardized incidence ratio of about
0.72, consistent with background rates. On that evidence the FDA removed the
boxed warning in 2020, while keeping the precautions for higher-risk groups and
the 2-year caution. It is a useful case study in how a striking animal signal
can fail to translate to humans.

## How To Evaluate A Teriparatide Claim

Because teriparatide is a genuine peptide drug, online claims often borrow its
credibility to sell unrelated products. A few questions separate signal from
noise.

First, is the claim about the approved indication, namely high-fracture-risk
osteoporosis, or is it stretching the peptide toward generic "anti-aging,"
joint repair or athletic recovery uses the label does not support?

Second, does it respect the intermittent-dosing biology? Any pitch implying that
more PTH exposure is simply better has the pharmacology backwards.

Third, does it mention the follow-on antiresorptive? Teriparatide gains erode
after stopping, so a serious discussion includes what comes next.

Fourth, how does it handle the osteosarcoma history? Overstating it ("causes
cancer") or hiding it both signal a low-quality source; the accurate version is
a rat finding not confirmed in human surveillance.

Fifth, is it citing the label and randomized trials, or vendor copy? As with
other approved peptides like [octreotide](/peptides/octreotide-peptide) and
[teduglutide](/peptides/teduglutide-peptide), prescribing information and
peer-reviewed trials are the standard, not supplement-shop blogs.

## Bottom Line

Teriparatide is a real, approved peptide medicine and one of the original
bone-building osteoporosis drugs. As recombinant PTH(1-34), delivered as a
once-daily subcutaneous injection, it uses intermittent PTH1 receptor signaling
to stimulate osteoblasts and add bone, with randomized trial evidence showing
substantial reductions in vertebral and nonvertebral fractures in high-risk
patients.

The same biology sets its boundaries. It depends on short daily exposure, is
labeled for specific high-fracture-risk populations, is generally followed by an
antiresorptive, and carries a distinctive safety history in which a rat-derived
osteosarcoma boxed warning was ultimately removed after large human
surveillance. Teriparatide is a precise endocrine tool for osteoporosis, not a
general wellness or recovery peptide, and it should only be used under medical
supervision.

Reconstitution means adding a sterile diluent to a vial so the contents become a
measured solution. In peptide searches, people usually mean adding
bacteriostatic water or another specified diluent to a lyophilized peptide vial.

The workflow below covers what to check before mixing, how the math
works, how to avoid contamination, how to label the vial, and when to stop and
ask a pharmacist or prescriber.

Use the [peptide reconstitution calculator](/calculators) for arithmetic, the
[bacteriostatic water guide](/peptides/bacteriostatic-water-guide) for diluent
details, the [peptide storage guide](/peptides/peptide-storage-guide) for
temperature and discard rules, and
[how to inject peptides safely](/peptides/how-to-inject-peptides-safely) for
alcohol wiping, vial entry and sharps handling.

## Reconstitution Math

The basic formula is simple:

<table className="protocol-table">
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Input</th>
      <th>Meaning</th>
      <th>Example</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Vial amount</td>
      <td>Total peptide or medication in the vial.</td>
      <td>10 mg</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Diluent volume</td>
      <td>Total liquid added to the vial.</td>
      <td>2 mL</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Concentration</td>
      <td>Vial amount divided by diluent volume.</td>
      <td>10 mg / 2 mL = 5 mg/mL</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Dose volume</td>
      <td>Target amount divided by concentration.</td>
      <td>1 mg / 5 mg/mL = 0.2 mL</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Syringe units</td>
      <td>On U-100 syringes, 1 mL equals 100 units.</td>
      <td>0.2 mL = 20 units</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

Do the math before mixing. If the calculated draw volume is too tiny to measure
accurately or too large for the syringe, ask the prescriber or pharmacist before
changing the water volume yourself.

## Before You Mix

Check the vial and diluent:

- The vial label matches the product you intended to use.
- The product is meant to be reconstituted.
- The diluent is the exact type specified.
- The vial is not expired and the seal is intact.
- The powder or liquid appearance matches the instructions.
- You know whether the vial is single-dose or multi-dose.
- You know the storage and discard instructions after mixing.

Stop if the vial has unexpected particles, discoloration, broken seal, moisture
where there should be dry powder, or unclear instructions.

## Supplies

Gather:

- Product vial.
- Correct sterile diluent.
- New sterile syringe and needle for adding diluent.
- New sterile syringe and needle for each later withdrawal.
- Alcohol pads.
- Clean label or marker.
- Sharps container.

The CDC's safe injection guidance is blunt on this point: every vial access
needs sterile equipment. A preservative in a multi-dose vial is not a license to
reuse needles or syringes.

## Step-by-Step Reconstitution Protocol

<table className="protocol-table">
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Order</th>
      <th>Action</th>
      <th>Reason</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>1</td>
      <td>Wash hands and work on a clean, dry surface.</td>
      <td>Reduces contamination risk before supplies are opened.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>2</td>
      <td>Confirm vial, diluent, water volume and storage instructions.</td>
      <td>Wrong diluent or wrong volume changes concentration and safety.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>3</td>
      <td>Wipe the diluent vial stopper and peptide vial stopper with alcohol. Let both dry.</td>
      <td>The needle passes through the stopper into the vial contents.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>4</td>
      <td>Draw the planned diluent volume with a new sterile syringe.</td>
      <td>Using a measured syringe makes the final concentration predictable.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>5</td>
      <td>Insert the needle into the peptide vial and add diluent slowly, preferably down the vial wall.</td>
      <td>Gentle addition reduces foaming and direct impact on the powder.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>6</td>
      <td>Remove the needle and dispose of it in a sharps container.</td>
      <td>The reconstitution needle is no longer sterile after use.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>7</td>
      <td>Gently swirl or roll the vial until dissolved, unless the instructions say otherwise.</td>
      <td>Many peptides and proteins should not be shaken aggressively.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>8</td>
      <td>Inspect the solution for unexpected cloudiness, particles, color change or precipitation.</td>
      <td>Do not use a solution that looks wrong for that product.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>9</td>
      <td>Label the vial with date mixed, concentration, diluent and discard date.</td>
      <td>Prevents later dose and storage mistakes.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>10</td>
      <td>Store exactly as the label, pharmacy or manufacturer states.</td>
      <td>Storage rules differ by product and diluent.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

## Label the Vial Clearly

For temperature, travel, light exposure and discard decisions, use the
[peptide storage guide](/peptides/peptide-storage-guide) alongside the product
label or pharmacy instructions.

Write:

- Date and time reconstituted.
- Total vial amount.
- Diluent type and volume.
- Final concentration.
- Storage condition.
- Discard date.

## Single-Dose vs Multi-Dose Vials

<table className="protocol-table">
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Vial type</th>
      <th>What it means</th>
      <th>Practical rule</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Single-dose</td>
      <td>Made for one use after entry.</td>
      <td>Do not save leftovers or combine leftovers for later use.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Multi-dose</td>
      <td>Labeled for repeated withdrawals and often contains preservative.</td>
      <td>Date when first punctured and discard by label/pharmacy guidance.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Unknown</td>
      <td>You cannot tell from forum language or assumptions.</td>
      <td>Ask the pharmacist or prescriber before using it like a multi-dose vial.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

CDC guidance says opened multi-dose vials are generally dated and discarded
within 28 days unless the manufacturer gives a different date. That is a
healthcare standard, not permission to ignore a shorter product-specific
discard date.

## What Not to Do

Avoid these:

- Do not use tap water, drinking water or an unspecified liquid.
- Do not mix two products together unless a pharmacist confirms compatibility.
- Do not save single-dose vial leftovers.
- Do not pool leftovers from multiple vials.
- Do not enter a vial with a used syringe or needle.
- Do not shake aggressively unless instructions say to shake.
- Do not store a reconstituted solution longer than the label allows.
- Do not assume bacteriostatic water makes contamination impossible.

## Troubleshooting

<table className="protocol-table">
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Issue</th>
      <th>What it may mean</th>
      <th>What to do</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Foam</td>
      <td>Diluent was added too forcefully or vial was shaken.</td>
      <td>Let it settle. Do not use if the product instructions say the appearance is unacceptable.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Particles</td>
      <td>Incomplete dissolving, precipitation or contamination.</td>
      <td>Do not inject until a pharmacist or prescriber confirms it is acceptable.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Cloudiness</td>
      <td>May be normal for some products but wrong for others.</td>
      <td>Compare with the product instructions. Stop if unexpected.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Wrong water volume added</td>
      <td>Final concentration changed.</td>
      <td>Do not guess. Recalculate and confirm with the pharmacist or prescriber.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

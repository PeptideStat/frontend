*for you*, given your goals, what your insurance covers and what your
prescriber thinks is safe. But the clinical trial data does give a clean
ranking on average weight loss, and that's where most people want to
start.

## The honest ranking, by trial weight loss

| Drug (brand) | Active ingredient | Average weight loss in pivotal trial | Trial length | FDA-approved for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zepbound** | Tirzepatide 15 mg | **~21% body weight** (SURMOUNT-1) | 72 weeks | Chronic weight management; OSA |
| **Mounjaro** | Tirzepatide 15 mg | ~21% body weight (off-label) | 72 weeks | Type 2 diabetes |
| **Wegovy** | Semaglutide 2.4 mg | **~15% body weight** (STEP-1) | 68 weeks | Chronic weight management; CV risk reduction; MASH |
| **Ozempic** | Semaglutide 2.0 mg | ~12–15% body weight (off-label) | 68 weeks | Type 2 diabetes |
| **Saxenda** | Liraglutide 3.0 mg | ~8% body weight | 56 weeks | Chronic weight management |

These numbers are averages from the pivotal weight-management trials.
Individual results vary substantially. Retatrutide, the next generation
"triple agonist", has reported even higher numbers in Phase 2 but is
**not yet approved** — see our
[Retatrutide guide](/peptides/retatrutide) for what its trials show.

## How each one works

**Semaglutide (Wegovy / Ozempic / Rybelsus)** mimics the GLP-1 incretin
hormone. It binds to GLP-1 receptors, slows gastric emptying, increases
satiety and improves insulin response. Single-receptor agonist. Weekly
injection (Wegovy and Ozempic) or daily oral (Rybelsus).

**Tirzepatide (Zepbound / Mounjaro)** is a dual agonist — it activates
**both** GLP-1 and GIP receptors. Adding the GIP arm appears to amplify
the appetite and metabolic effects, which is why tirzepatide outperforms
semaglutide on average weight loss in trials. Weekly injection.

**Liraglutide (Saxenda)** is the earlier-generation GLP-1, daily
injection. Same mechanism as semaglutide but shorter half-life and
generally less weight loss.

## When each one is the right answer

### Zepbound (tirzepatide)

**Best for:** Highest average weight loss in trials. Newly approved for
obstructive sleep apnea in adults with obesity, which broadens the
clinical indications. Dose escalation runs 2.5 → 15 mg weekly.

**Watch outs:** Most likely to cause GI side effects (nausea, vomiting,
diarrhea) — they're typically transient but can be intense during dose
escalation. Manufacturer copay programs are favorable for commercially
insured patients; cash pay is reduced via LillyDirect vials.

### Wegovy (semaglutide)

**Best for:** The strongest *cardiovascular* evidence in the class — the
SELECT trial showed a 20% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular
events. Also approved for MASH (metabolic-associated steatohepatitis)
fibrosis. Often the right pick if cardiovascular risk reduction matters
on top of weight loss.

**Watch outs:** Lower average weight loss than Zepbound (~15% vs ~21%).
Same GI side-effect profile as Ozempic. Insurance coverage for weight-
loss use is variable but improving.

### Mounjaro (tirzepatide)

**Best for:** People with type 2 diabetes. Same molecule as Zepbound but
labeled for glycemic control. Patients with T2D usually have far better
insurance coverage for Mounjaro than for Zepbound.

**Watch outs:** Off-label use for weight loss without T2D is common but
insurance-unsupported. Side-effect profile identical to Zepbound.

### Ozempic (semaglutide)

**Best for:** People with type 2 diabetes who want a weekly injection.
The original blockbuster.

**Watch outs:** Off-label use for weight loss is the most expensive
brand-name path on cash pay because manufacturer savings cards are
typically tied to the FDA-approved use. If your goal is purely weight
loss, Wegovy is the cost-friendlier match.

### Saxenda (liraglutide)

**Best for:** Daily-injection comfort, longer track record (approved 2014),
and a useful option when supply of newer agents is constrained.

**Watch outs:** Lower average weight loss than the weekly options. Daily
dosing is an adherence burden some patients dislike.

## Side-effect profile, head to head

All GLP-1s share the same dominant side-effect pattern — gastrointestinal,
dose-related, mostly transient:

| Side effect | Common | Less common | When it usually occurs |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nausea | All | – | First 4–8 weeks; during dose escalation |
| Vomiting | All | – | Same |
| Diarrhea / constipation | All | – | Variable |
| Reflux / belching | All | – | Throughout |

## Who probably shouldn't take any GLP-1

- Personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN
  syndrome type 2
- History of pancreatitis
- Severe gastroparesis already
- Pregnancy or planning pregnancy in the near term (washout periods apply)
- Active eating disorder
- Type 1 diabetes (different physiology; not a GLP-1 indication)

## Decision summary

- **Want maximum average weight loss?** Zepbound (tirzepatide).
- **Want the strongest cardiovascular protection?** Wegovy (semaglutide).
- **Have type 2 diabetes?** Mounjaro or Ozempic, whichever your insurance
  covers better.
- **Want a longer-track-record option / daily injection?** Saxenda.
- **Want maximum potential efficacy and are willing to wait?** The next

## FAQ

**Which GLP-1 produces the most weight loss?**
By trial average, tirzepatide (Zepbound / Mounjaro) at the 15 mg dose
produces the most — about 21% body weight reduction over 72 weeks.

**Is Zepbound really better than Wegovy?**
On average weight loss in trials, yes. On cardiovascular outcomes,
Wegovy has the better evidence so far.

**Can I switch GLP-1s mid-course?**
Yes, prescribers commonly switch patients for insurance, supply or side-
effect reasons. Dose conversion is non-trivial — it has to be managed by
the prescriber.

**Is there a "best GLP-1 for women"?**
No clinically meaningful sex-specific difference among the approved
drugs. Trial efficacy and safety apply across sexes.

**What's the best GLP-1 if I have diabetes and want to lose weight?**
Mounjaro for tirzepatide users, Ozempic for semaglutide users — both
treat T2D and produce weight loss, but the cost and coverage equation
strongly favors whichever is on your formulary.
